##### Spectroscopy
- Measurement and study of the **spectrum** of electromagnetic radiation from a celestial object
- Spectra can be *continuous*, have *absorption lines* or have *emission lines*. Lines are associated with specific elements present in the source
- Information is gained through eg. the ratio of line strengths, the apparent vs known positions of lines, the total radiation and *peak emission wavelength* $\lambda_{max}$
- Example uses:
	- Finding temperature, total luminosity and radius of *black bodies* like stars
	- Observing the *21 cm line* emission of hydrogen atoms
	- Finding the *redshift* of galaxies
	- Studying the atmosphere of planets


##### Photometry
- Measurement and study of the **flux**/intensity or **apparent magnitude** of light emitted by celestial objects
- Light is passed through a set of *photometric bandpass filters*, and intensity is only measured at those specific *wavelengths*
- **Colours** are the magnitude differences between filters and are related to *temperature*
- **Broad-band/narrow-band photometry** refers to the width of the *frequency*/wavelength range of the filters used in observations
	- Board-band photometry is used for *continuum* observations and is more sensitive. The common *UBVRI* filters have bandwidths of ~100 nm each; broadband filters in the radio spectrum have widths ~100 MHz
	- Narrow-band photometry is used for *spectral line* observations, which require filter bandwidths that match the frequency range of the line so as not to pass excessive continuum emission or emission from other nearby lines. Bandwidths are ~5 nm in the optical spectrum; bandwidths are ~4 MHz in the radio spectrum
